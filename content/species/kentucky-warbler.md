# Kentucky Warbler

## Look for
A **bright yellow throat and chest** with **black sideburns** extending from the eye down the side of the neck — like a little bandit mask that leaks downward. **Olive-green back**, yellow belly, brief **yellow spectacles** around the eye.

Males and females look similar (females slightly duller sideburns).

**Size**: ~5.25" — small warbler.

## Listen for
- **Song**: a loud, rolling **"churry-churry-churry-churry"** — a series of 5–7 identical phrases, each a clear two-syllable whistle. Carries well through forest.
- **Call**: a sharp **"chip"** from low cover.

Similar to Carolina Wren's "teakettle" but **slower, richer, more flute-like**.

## Where in Memphis
**Present but uncommon** in:
- **Wolf River Greenway** — moist bottomland hollows
- **Meeman-Shelby Forest** — ravine floors

Requires **moist ground-level understory** — dense jewelweed, ferns, and saplings. Memphis bottomland forests with consistent spring soil moisture host them.

## Why Montgomery Bell is ideal
Kentucky Warblers live **on the forest floor** of **moist hollow bottoms** — the low, damp ground between ridges. Montgomery Bell's **hollow floors** (between oak-hickory ridges) have spring seeps, jewelweed patches, and dense saplings — perfect KEWA habitat.

Look along **Creech Hollow Trail** floor, and where trails dip into ravines.

## Behavior
- **Ground and low-understory foragers.** Hop like thrushes through leaf-litter, flip leaves, and snap up caterpillars from low branches.
- **Stay low** — rarely go above 10 ft.
- **Males sing from elevated perches** (up to 20 ft) but feed low.
- **Well-camouflaged nesters** — build cup nests on the ground in leaf-litter mounds or at the base of saplings.

## Story

### The Kentucky misnomer (pattern again)
**Alexander Wilson** (again) named this bird from a specimen collected in **Kentucky** in 1811. The bird isn't Kentucky-specific — it breeds across the Southeast — but the name stuck. Same pattern as Nashville Warbler and Tennessee Warbler: bird named after the state it was collected in, not where it's most common.

### The "churry" challenge
Kentucky Warbler's song is **often confused with Carolina Wren** and **Louisiana Waterthrush**. Distinguishing them in a Middle TN hollow:
| Species | Rhythm |
|---|---|
| Kentucky Warbler | **Steady 5–7 phrases**, each identical |
| Carolina Wren | **3 phrases**, then done (faster) |
| Louisiana Waterthrush | **3 slurred intro notes**, then a chatter |

### Cowbird parasitism
Kentucky Warbler nests are **heavily parasitized by Brown-headed Cowbirds** in fragmented forests. In large contiguous blocks like Montgomery Bell, cowbird pressure is lower — which helps populations persist.

## Fun facts
- They **winter from southern Mexico to Venezuela** in tropical forest understory.
- Their **scientific name _formosa_** means "beautiful" in Latin — a fair assessment.
- **Oldest known** wild Kentucky Warbler: **8+ years**.
- Males **sometimes hybridize** with Common Yellowthroats (same genus _Geothlypis_).
- They're **in slow decline** across most of their range — habitat fragmentation + cowbird parasitism.
