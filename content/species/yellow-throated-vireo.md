# Yellow-throated Vireo

## Look for
A **chunky olive-backed songbird** with a **bright yellow throat + chest**, **bold yellow spectacles** (eye-ring + line from bill to eye), and **two white wing bars**. Stockier and slower-moving than warblers.

The only Eastern vireo with **yellow underparts + wing bars**.

**Size**: ~5.5" — small songbird, sparrow-sized.

## Listen for
- **Song**: a **slow, burry, two-note phrase** — **"three-eight!... three-eight!"** or **"see-me... here I am"** — each phrase separated by a 2–3 second pause. Rolls and growls more than a Red-eyed Vireo's clearer notes.
- **Call**: a harsh **"chi-chi-chi-chi"** scold.

**Pace is the tell**: Yellow-throated Vireos **sing slowly**, leaving long silences between phrases. Red-eyed Vireos (same habitat) rarely stop.

## Where in Memphis
**Present but uncommon** in mature deciduous forests:
- **Overton Park Old Forest**
- **Wolf River Greenway** (canopy)
- **Meeman-Shelby Forest**

## Why Montgomery Bell is reliable
Yellow-throated Vireos need **mature, large-tree canopy** — oak-hickory forests with 80+ ft tall trees. Montgomery Bell's **mature hardwood ridges** provide this at scale. The **Montgomery Bell State Park birding page** explicitly highlights them as reliable breeders.

Listen in the **upper canopy** throughout the park, especially along forested ridge tops.

## Behavior
- **Canopy foragers** — work 30–60+ ft up in the upper tree layer.
- **Slow, deliberate movers** compared to warblers. They sidle along branches rather than darting.
- **Fork-built hanging nests** — construct gorgeous cup nests suspended from horizontal forks near canopy branch tips, decorated with spider silk and lichens.
- **Males sing constantly** from the canopy throughout breeding season.

## Story

### The vireo family differences
Memphis hosts **four regular vireos**:

| Species | Where | Key mark |
|---|---|---|
| **Red-eyed Vireo** | Canopy, everywhere | White eyebrow, red eye |
| **White-eyed Vireo** | Shrub-thickets | Yellow spectacles, white eye |
| **Yellow-throated Vireo** | Mature canopy | Yellow throat, wing bars |
| **Warbling Vireo** | Tall trees by water | Plain gray-green, no marks |

Yellow-throated is the **slowest singer** of the four and prefers **older forest**.

### Partners in Flight concern
Yellow-throated Vireos are **modestly declining** due to:
- **Loss of mature-canopy forest** in fragmented landscapes
- **Cowbird parasitism** in edge-heavy small forests

Large protected forests like Montgomery Bell are **strongholds**.

### The "three-eight" mnemonic
Every Middle TN birder learns this song via the **"three-eight"** mnemonic. Once you've heard it, the slow rhythm is unmistakable — even through dense canopy.

## Fun facts
- They **winter from southern Mexico to northern South America**.
- Their **scientific name _flavifrons_** means "yellow forehead" (though the throat is more striking).
- **Oldest known** wild Yellow-throated Vireo: **8+ years**.
- Nests are **engineering marvels** — hanging baskets held by rim attachments to a forked branch, spun from spider silk, moss, and bark fibers.
- The **Pine Warbler** in pine stands sings a similar slow trill — check the habitat to separate.
