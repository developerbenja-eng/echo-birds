# Mississippi Kite

## Look for
An elegant gray raptor with a **pale ghost-white head**, darker gray back and wings, and a **black, slightly forked tail**. From below the wings are long, pointed, and silvery with dark trailing edges. Red eyes. Adults look almost like they're made of smoke.

They fly like no other Memphis raptor: **buoyant, floating, circling high overhead on warm afternoons**, often catching dragonflies mid-air and eating them on the wing.

**Size**: ~14" body, ~3 ft wingspan — between a crow and a Red-tailed Hawk.

## Listen for
- A high, clear, **descending two-note whistle — "phee-phew"** — often given in flight.
- Near the nest, a sharp chatter when humans walk too close.

## Where in Memphis
**The defining raptor of Memphis summer.** Mississippi Kites arrive in early May and nest right in the heart of the city — tall pecans, oaks, and pines in Midtown neighborhoods, Downtown parks, and the Mississippi River bluffs. Look up from **Tom Lee Park, Overton Park, the I-240 loop, Cooper-Young porches, and Central Gardens backyards** on any hot June or July afternoon.

By mid-August, they're forming pre-migration kettles over the bluffs. By early September, they're gone — most to central South America.

## Behavior

- **Aerial hunters.** Kites catch cicadas, dragonflies, grasshoppers, and beetles on the wing. They'll also pluck bats from the sky at dusk and snatch small birds out of mid-air.
- **They eat as they fly.** Watch one circling with a dragonfly pinned in its talons, methodically tearing pieces free with its beak.
- **Colonial-ish nesters.** Loose groups of nests in a neighborhood are common. One good pecan can host a pair year after year.
- **Dive-bomb defenders.** During nesting (mid-June through July), kites will buzz humans who walk too close to the nest tree — close enough to feel the wind off their wings. They rarely make contact but it's unnerving. Memphis joggers know the feeling.

## Story & folklore

### The city that grew kites
Mississippi Kites were historically bottomland-forest birds of the Deep South's great river floodplains. As those forests were cut and cities grew, the kites pivoted — they now breed across Memphis **at higher densities than in the remaining bottomlands**. Urban Memphis, with its pecan-lined streets and open sky, is one of the great Mississippi Kite cities in the U.S.

The population in the Memphis metro is estimated in the **hundreds of pairs**, possibly over a thousand. Most residents have no idea.

### The "mosquito hawk" mix-up
In the Delta, people sometimes call kites "mosquito hawks," though actual mosquito hawks are dragonflies. Delta English runs on vibes.

### Dive-bombing the Little Leaguers
In the 1990s and 2000s, news stories periodically ran about Memphis Mississippi Kites attacking joggers, golfers, and Little League outfielders — always in late June, always near a nest. A few suburbs tried to relocate nests; wildlife officials mostly explained that the birds would leave on their own in three weeks.

## Fun facts

- Kites are **extreme long-distance migrants** — Memphis to Paraguay and back every year, roughly 10,000 miles round trip, in a 14-ounce bird.
- A Mississippi Kite's **wingbeat looks almost lazy** — they ride thermals so well they barely flap.
- They're one of the **only raptors that regularly eat bats**, grabbing them in the low light after sunset.
- Males and females look identical; young birds are streaky brown-and-white for their first year.
- They're in the same family as eagles and hawks (Accipitridae) but behave more like giant swallows.
- The scientific name **_mississippiensis_** was given in 1811 from specimens collected along the lower Mississippi — this bird is literally named after the river Memphis sits on.
