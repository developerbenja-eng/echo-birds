# Magnolia Warbler

## Look for
One of the **most striking spring warblers**: **black mask and chest streaks** on a **bright yellow body**, **white wing patch**, **gray cap**, and a **distinctive "half-and-half" black-and-white tail** (black at the tip, white at the base).

Females and fall birds are subtler but retain the yellow + tail pattern.

**Size**: ~5" — small warbler.

## Listen for
- **Song**: a short, musical, rising **"weta-weta-wetsee!"** — soft and unhurried compared to most warbler songs.

## Where in Memphis
**Migration only** — peaks in **mid-May** and **mid-September**. The Old Forest at Overton Park and Wolf River Greenway bottomlands are reliable.

## Behavior
- **Mid-story forager** — works 10–30 ft up in branches, often flicking wings and tail.
- **Tail-flashing** (like American Redstart) — shows off the distinctive black-tipped tail.
- **Very active** — moves constantly.

## Story

### The accidental name
**Alexander Wilson** (founder of American ornithology) shot the first specimen in 1810 **from a magnolia tree in Mississippi**. The bird doesn't prefer magnolias at all — but the name stuck. Wilson's scientific name **_magnolia_** now describes a species that breeds in **spruce-fir forests of Canada**, nowhere near a magnolia.

A perfect example of **accidental nomenclature**.

### "Maggie"
Birders universally shorten the name to "Maggie." Nobody says "Magnolia Warbler" after their first week of birding.

### The Memphis spring warbler list
Every Memphis birder's May checklist includes Maggie alongside American Redstart, Black-throated Green, Chestnut-sided, and a dozen other warbler-wave regulars. Finding a male Magnolia in full breeding plumage at Overton in mid-May is one of the **reliable joys** of Mid-South birding.

## Fun facts
- Their **half-and-half tail** is unique among Memphis warblers — a quick ID from underneath.
- They **winter in Central America** and the Caribbean, migrating through the Gulf of Mexico.
- Nest in **young conifer stands** in boreal forest — prefer trees 3–10 feet tall, not the old-growth.
- **Oldest known** wild Magnolia Warbler: **8+ years**.
