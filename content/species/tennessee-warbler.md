# Tennessee Warbler

## Look for
A **small, plain olive-green warbler** — clean white underparts, **no wing bars**, thin pointed bill, white **supercilium** (eyebrow stripe), grayish head. Deceptively subtle — more like a vireo than most warblers.

**Size**: ~4.75" — tiny, smaller than a sparrow.

## Listen for
- **Song**: a loud, staccato **three-part "tsip-tsip-tsip... swit-swit-swit... chew-chew-chew-chew"** — mechanical, staccato, accelerating.

## Where in Memphis
**Migration only.** Tennessee Warblers pass through in **late April–early May** and **mid-September–early October**. They breed in the boreal forest of Canada, winter in Central America — they **do not breed in Tennessee.**
- **Overton Park Old Forest**
- **Meeman-Shelby**, **Wolf River**
- **Shelby Farms** woodland edges

## Behavior
- **Canopy gleaners** — work branches and leaf clusters 20–60 ft up.
- **Nectar drinkers** in winter — important pollinators of tropical flowers in Mexico and Central America.
- **Among the most abundant warblers** passing through Memphis in May.

## Story

### The misleading name
**Alexander Wilson** collected the first specimen **near Nashville** on the Cumberland River in 1811 — on migration, not a breeding bird. He named it Tennessee Warbler, and the name stuck even though **no Tennessee Warblers breed in Tennessee.**

Classic example of **collection-site nomenclature** creating permanent confusion. (See also: Magnolia Warbler.)

### The spruce-budworm connection
Their populations **boom and bust** with outbreaks of **spruce budworm** in the Canadian boreal forest. A boom year for budworms = a bumper year of Tennessee Warblers passing through Memphis.

### The Memphis spring list
Every mid-May Overton Park birder finds several Tennessee Warblers mixed with American Redstarts, Magnolia Warblers, and Chestnut-sided Warblers. The **song is often the first ID** — look is hard, song is unmistakable.

## Fun facts
- They **winter in shade-coffee plantations** — a major conservation argument for buying shade-grown coffee.
- A Tennessee Warbler weighs about **9 grams** — less than two nickels.
- They migrate **across the Gulf of Mexico** at night, nonstop.
- **Oldest known** wild Tennessee Warbler: **7+ years**.
