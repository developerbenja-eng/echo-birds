# Red Clay State Historic Park

## Why Red Clay?

The **last seat of Cherokee government** before the Trail of Tears (1838). Red Clay served as the Cherokee national capital after Georgia expelled them from New Echota. The park preserves the council spring, replica council house, and 263 acres of woodland — a deeply significant historic site.

Birding is a secondary activity here — the history is the draw.

## Birding

- **Eastern Towhee** — brushy woodland edges, "drink-your-tea"
- **Great Crested Flycatcher** — woodland canopy
- **Eastern Bluebird** — open areas
- **Northern Parula** — forest canopy

## The Council Spring

The **Blue Hole Spring** emerges from the ground at 168,000 gallons/day — the sacred spring where Cherokee council meetings were held. It's one of the most historically significant natural features in Tennessee.

## Logistics

- **Entry**: Free
- **Museum**: Excellent Cherokee history exhibits
- **Trails**: Short (< 2 miles)
- **eBird hotspot**: Red Clay State Historic Park

**Best strategy**: 1-hour historic visit + casual birding. 30 min from Chattanooga.
