# Frozen Head State Park

## Why Frozen Head?

**The highest peak on the Cumberland Plateau** (3,324 ft) with **old-growth cove forest** — creating elevation-dependent birding that mimics the Great Smokies but is more accessible and less crowded. Frozen Head is one of the **top warbler destinations in Tennessee**, with **species from three elevation zones** in one park: plateau-bottom, mid-slope, and mountaintop.

The upper elevations support **Black-throated Blue Warbler, Canada Warbler**, and — the holy grail — **Golden-winged Warbler**, species normally associated with the Blue Ridge Mountains.

This is also the home of the **Barkley Marathons** — the infamous ultramarathon where most years no one finishes.

## The landscape

24,000 acres rising from 1,300 ft to 3,324 ft, with:

- **Old-growth cove hardwood** — massive tulip poplar, hemlock, and white oak. Some trees 300+ years old
- **Rhododendron-laurel understory** — dense evergreen shrub layer at mid-to-upper elevations
- **Upper-elevation spruce-like forest** — near the summit, fir-birch-beech transitional forest
- **Rocky streams** — clear water flowing through forested ravines
- **Mine reclamation areas** — old coal mine sites reverting to young forest (Golden-winged Warbler habitat)
- **Panther Branch** — main stream through the park

## Birding routes

### Panther Branch Trail → Chimney Top Trail (6 mi round-trip — strenuous)
The signature climb from 1,300 ft to Chimney Top (2,919 ft). **The warbler elevator.**

**Lower elevations (1,300–2,000 ft)**:
- **Louisiana Waterthrush** — Panther Branch stream edges
- **Hooded Warbler** — dense understory
- **Wood Thrush** — cove forest
- **Northern Parula** — canopy

**Mid-slope (2,000–2,600 ft)**:
- **Black-throated Green Warbler** — hemlock zones
- **Worm-eating Warbler** — steep leafy slopes
- **Cerulean Warbler** — canopy of tall hardwoods

**Upper elevations (2,600–3,300 ft)**:
- **Black-throated Blue Warbler** — rhododendron + dense understory (breeding)
- **Canada Warbler** — rhododendron thickets near streams (breeding)
- **Dark-eyed Junco** — near summit (year-round)

### South Old Mac Trail (4 mi round-trip — moderate)
Follows a different ridge with less elevation gain.

- **Cerulean Warbler** — ridge-top canopy
- **Scarlet Tanager** — upland forest
- **Great Crested Flycatcher** — canopy calling

### Judge Branch Trail (2 mi — moderate)
Stream-bottom trail with less climbing.

- **Louisiana Waterthrush** — stream corridor
- **Hooded Warbler** — understory
- **Acadian Flycatcher** — hemlock ravine

### Mine reclamation areas
Old coal mine sites with early-successional vegetation.

- **Golden-winged Warbler** — **RARE** but present. Young forest + shrub edges above 2,500 ft. Listen for the buzzy "bee-bzz-bzz-bzz." This is one of the southernmost breeding locations in the Appalachians
- **Prairie Warbler** — shrubby areas
- **Eastern Towhee** — brushy edges
- **Indigo Bunting** — open edges

## Seasonal strategy

**May–June** (★★★★★): **Peak season.** All elevation zones active. Every warbler singing. Rhododendron blooming (late May–June) — spectacular. Best window for upper-elevation specialties.

**April** (★★★★): Early breeders on territory at lower elevations. Upper elevations still cold. Migration adds diversity.

**July** (★★★): Still singing but quieter. Heat at low elevations but cool at summit. Dawn + dusk.

**September** (★★★): Fall migration through. Confusing fall warblers.

**Winter** (★★): Quiet. Dark-eyed Junco near summit. Pileated Woodpecker. Snow possible at elevation.

## The elevation birding concept

Frozen Head compresses **multiple biomes** into a single hike:

| Elevation | Biome equivalent | Key species |
|-----------|-----------------|-------------|
| 1,300 ft | **Memphis-level** | Waterthrush, Hooded, Wood Thrush |
| 2,000 ft | **Highland Rim** | Cerulean, Black-throated Green |
| 2,600 ft | **Southern Appalachian** | Black-throated Blue, Canada |
| 3,300 ft | **Northern hardwood** | Dark-eyed Junco, Veery |

You literally walk from Memphis bird communities to New England bird communities in 3 miles.

## Logistics

- **Hours**: Sunrise to sunset
- **Entry**: Free
- **Camping**: 20 sites (basic, first-come first-served)
- **Trails**: 50+ miles
- **Cell service**: Poor to none (Morgan County backcountry)
- **eBird hotspot**: Frozen Head State Park
- **Warning**: Upper trails are strenuous with significant elevation gain. Bring water, layers.

**Best strategy**: Full day. Start at dawn on Panther Branch → Chimney Top Trail. Climb slowly, birding each elevation zone. Turn around when satisfied or at the summit. 5-6 hours for the full climb. Camp to get dawn chorus from inside the forest.

## Pairing

- **Cumberland Mountain** (30 min south) — CCC-era plateau park
- **Norris Dam** (1 hr east) — TVA reservoir birding
