# Radnor Lake State Natural Area

## Why Radnor Lake?

**Nashville's birding crown jewel** and arguably the **best single birding location in Middle Tennessee**. With **240+ species recorded**, Radnor Lake packs extraordinary diversity into 1,368 acres of forested hills surrounding an 85-acre lake just 6 miles from downtown Nashville.

**What makes it special**: a pristine lake surrounded by mature deciduous forest on steep ridges, managed as a **State Natural Area** (Tennessee's highest protection level). No swimming, no boating, no fishing, no off-trail hiking — the wildlife has it easy here. The result is **unusually approachable birds** in a forest that feels wild despite being inside a metro area of 2 million.

This is where Nashville birders go when they want to see the most species in a single morning.

## The landscape

Radnor Lake sits in a **narrow valley** between two forested ridges in the Nashville Basin. The lake was created in 1914 by the L&N Railroad as a water supply — and then nearly lost to development in the 1970s before a citizen campaign saved it.

**Key habitats**:
- **85-acre lake** with wooded shoreline — wintering waterfowl, Great Blue Heron rookery
- **Mature deciduous cove forest** on steep north-facing slopes — high canopy warbler habitat
- **Ridge-top oak-hickory forest** — drier, more open understory
- **Ravine streams** — Louisiana Waterthrush territory
- **Wetland edges** on the lake's south shore
- **Rocky cedar glades** on south-facing slopes (small but present)

The elevation change from lake (600 ft) to ridge (900 ft) creates **microhabitat diversity** unusual for such a small area.

## Birding routes

### Lake Trail (2 mi loop — flat, easy)
The signature route. Follows the south shore of the lake through mature forest.

**Target species along Lake Trail**:
- **Northern Parula** — common in canopy above trail, singing March–August
- **Yellow-throated Warbler** — high sycamores along the lake, arrives mid-March
- **Barred Owl** — resident, often vocal even in daylight; check cove forest
- **Pileated Woodpecker** — loud calling, look for rectangular excavations
- **Wood Thrush** — breeds in cove forest, ethereal flute-song at dawn
- **Great Blue Heron** — rookery visible from east end of lake
- **Bald Eagle** — resident pair, often perched in tall lakeside trees

**Winter**: scan lake for Ring-necked Duck, Hooded Merganser, Bufflehead, Pied-billed Grebe.

### South Cove Trail (1.5 mi — moderate)
Connects to Lake Trail on the quieter south side. Less foot traffic = more birds.

- **Hooded Warbler** — dense understory breeding habitat
- **Red-shouldered Hawk** — nests in tall trees near cove
- **Great Crested Flycatcher** — WHEEP calls from canopy May–August

### Ganier Ridge Trail (2.7 mi — strenuous)
Climbs to the ridge above the lake. More work, but often productive.

- **Yellow-throated Vireo** — oak canopy along ridgetop
- **Scarlet Tanager** — breeding in upland forest
- **Broad-winged Hawk** — soaring above ridgeline in migration

### Otter Creek Road (paved, east entrance)
Best for dawn birding when the park opens. Walk the road before crowds arrive.

- **Louisiana Waterthrush** — Otter Creek crossing, arrives late March
- **Eastern Phoebe** — bridge undersides
- **Northern Parula** — abundant in creekside forest

## Seasonal strategy

**March** (★★★★★): Yellow-throated Warbler arrives mid-month (often the first Setophaga). Louisiana Waterthrush on streams. Early migrant push begins. Waterfowl still on lake.

**April** (★★★★★): **Peak migration**. Warbler waves crash through — 20+ warbler species possible in a single morning during a good fallout. Northern Parula, Hooded Warbler, Wood Thrush all on territory. Wildflowers carpet the forest floor.

**May** (★★★★★): Breeding season peak. Great Crested Flycatcher, Yellow-throated Vireo, Red-eyed Vireo all singing. Late migrants (Blackpoll Warbler, Bay-breasted Warbler) pass through. Dawn chorus is extraordinary.

**September–October** (★★★★): Fall migration. Warblers in confusing fall plumage — bring patience. Thrushes moving through. Hawk migration visible from ridgetop.

**November–February** (★★★): Wintering waterfowl on lake. Bald Eagle active. Winter finch irruptions (some years). Barred Owl vocal. Pileated Woodpecker conspicuous. Quiet forest walks.

**June–August** (★★): Breeding birds on territory but heat + humidity + silence after dawn. Go early or not at all.

## What Radnor has that Memphis doesn't

Radnor Lake's combination of **elevation change + pristine lake + mature cove forest** creates conditions rare in the flat Mississippi Delta landscape around Memphis:

| Feature | Radnor Lake | Memphis parks |
|---------|------------|---------------|
| **Elevation range** | 300 ft change | Flat |
| **Lake** | Pristine, no boats | Reservoirs/rivers |
| **Cove forest** | Mature, protected | Fragmented |
| **Barred Owl** | Very common | Present but sparse |
| **Warbler diversity** | 30+ species/year | 20+ species/year |
| **Crowds** | Can be heavy on trails | Generally lighter |

## Logistics

- **Hours**: Sunrise to sunset, year-round
- **Entry**: Free, no fee
- **Dogs**: Not allowed
- **Parking**: Limited — fills early on weekends. Arrive by 7am in spring/fall
- **Trails**: 7 miles total, well-maintained
- **Facilities**: Visitor center with restrooms, nature exhibits
- **eBird hotspot**: Radnor Lake State Natural Area (one of Tennessee's most-reported hotspots)
- **Cell service**: Good (Davidson County)

**Best strategy**: Arrive at sunrise, walk Otter Creek Road → Lake Trail → South Cove. 3-4 hours. Bring binoculars + water. In spring migration, check eBird the night before for reports of fallout conditions.
