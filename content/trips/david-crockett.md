# David Crockett State Park

## Why David Crockett?

**Shoal Creek + upland hardwood forest** in the scenic hills of Lawrence County. Named for Davy Crockett, who operated a gristmill, powder mill, and distillery on Shoal Creek near this site before heading to Texas. The park preserves **1,319 acres of rich Southern Appalachian-influenced forest** with excellent breeding warblers.

The combination of **creek-bottom habitat + upland forest + brushy edges** supports a strong summer breeding bird community.

## The landscape

- **Shoal Creek** — clear, rocky creek with excellent riparian habitat
- **40-acre lake** — impounded for CCC-era recreation, attracts waterfowl
- **Mature upland hardwood forest** — oak, hickory, tulip poplar, beech
- **Dense understory** — mountain laurel + rhododendron in ravines
- **Brushy edges** — old field margins + power-line cuts

## Birding routes

### Shoal Creek Trail (3 mi — moderate)
Follows the creek through bottomland forest. Best birding in the park.

- **Wood Thrush** — ethereal flute song at dawn in cove forest
- **Warbling Vireo** — sycamores along the creek
- **Louisiana Waterthrush** — rocky creek edges
- **Northern Parula** — canopy above trail

### Tall Trees Nature Trail (1 mi — easy)
Short loop through mature forest near the campground.

- **Great Crested Flycatcher** — canopy calling
- **Hooded Warbler** — dense understory patches
- **Kentucky Warbler** — ground-level in damp forest

### Lake Loop (1.5 mi — easy)
Circles the CCC-era lake.

- **Belted Kingfisher** — resident on lake
- **Wood Duck** — nesting in lakeside cavities
- **Eastern Towhee** — brushy edges around lake

## Seasonal strategy

**May–June** (★★★★): Peak breeding. All target species singing. Best window.

**April** (★★★): Early breeders arriving. Migration adds diversity.

**July** (★★★): Still singing but quieter. Dawn only.

**Winter** (★★): Quiet. Woodpeckers, Winter sparrows.
