# Montgomery Bell State Park — Day Trip Guide

> 3,700 acres of rolling hardwood forest + streams + lakes in Dickson County, Middle TN — about 40 miles west of Nashville, 180 miles east of Memphis (3 hr drive).

---

## Why it's different from Memphis

Memphis sits on the **Mississippi alluvial plain** at ~300 ft elevation. The habitat is **bottomland cypress-tupelo swamp**, flat river floodplain, and Delta bluffs. Our birds reflect that: Prothonotary Warblers in flooded woods, American White Pelicans on the big river, Mississippi Kites over downtown.

Montgomery Bell is on the **Western Highland Rim** — the tail end of the Interior Low Plateau. It's **hilly, 700–900 ft elevation**, with:
- **Mature oak-hickory ridges** and dry slopes
- **Hemlock ravines** and moist hollow floors
- **Rocky, gravel-bottom streams** (not Delta sloughs)
- **Small pine stands** scattered through
- **3 small lakes** (Acorn, Creech, Woodhaven)

This is **classic "Eastern interior forest" habitat** — the kind of place that hosts a bird community you won't see at Overton or Wolf River. Species here are tuned to **upland deciduous forest with slopes, streams, and ridges** — not swamps.

---

## April timing

You're arriving **late April** — the bullseye for Middle TN birding:
- **Year-round residents** are singing at peak
- **Early summer breeders** have arrived (Louisiana Waterthrush, Pine Warbler, Yellow-throated Warbler)
- **Late-April arrivals** are streaming in (Hooded Warbler, Wood Thrush, Scarlet Tanager, Acadian Flycatcher, Kentucky Warbler, Worm-eating Warbler)
- **Wild Turkey** gobbling is at its spring peak
- **Warbler migration** is kicking off for passage species

The forest is **loud**. Dawn chorus at Montgomery Bell in late April is the Middle TN equivalent of Memphis's Overton Park Old Forest warbler wave — probably even richer.

## Target species you won't find in Memphis (or rarely)

These are the species that make Montgomery Bell worth the drive. Eight new profiles written for this trip:

### Mature interior forest
- **[Wood Thrush](../species/wood-thrush.md)** — fluted, ethereal song; late-April arrival; true forest-interior specialist
- **[Hooded Warbler](../species/hooded-warbler.md)** — male has a black hood; dense shaded understory; "weeta-weeta-weet-tee-oh"
- **[Worm-eating Warbler](../species/worm-eating-warbler.md)** — dry ridges with mountain laurel; buzzy insect-like trill; easy to miss
- **[Kentucky Warbler](../species/kentucky-warbler.md)** — moist hollow floor; "churry-churry-churry"; prefers hollows with jewelweed

### Canopy + flycatching
- **[Yellow-throated Vireo](../species/yellow-throated-vireo.md)** — mature canopy; burry "three-eight, three-eight"; bright yellow spectacles
- **[Acadian Flycatcher](../species/acadian-flycatcher.md)** — explosive "pee-ZAY!" from hemlock ravines and creeksides

### Streams
- **[Louisiana Waterthrush](../species/louisiana-waterthrush.md)** — rocky stream specialist; walks the bank bobbing; loud ringing song; one of the earliest migrants (already singing by late March)

### Upland game
- **[Wild Turkey](../species/wild-turkey.md)** — year-round; **April is peak gobbling month**; you'll almost certainly hear them at dawn

## Trail picks for birding

Montgomery Bell has **~20 miles of trails**. Best birding paths:

### Montgomery Bell Trail (the long one, ~11 miles loop)
- Passes through mature hardwoods + past the lakes
- Good for mixed forest species
- ⚠️ Portions closed March 2026 through ~May 2026 for timber salvage after November 2025 storms — check park office

### Creech Hollow Trail
- Hollow-bottom ravines + creek + hemlocks
- Classic Kentucky Warbler, Acadian Flycatcher, Louisiana Waterthrush habitat
- ⚠️ Also partially closed for salvage work

### Jim Bailey Nature Trail (short, ~1 mile loop)
- Easy family walk near the park office
- Good for chickadees, titmice, nuthatches, thrushes

### Lake trails (Acorn Lake, Lake Woodhaven)
- Waterfowl in winter (Wood Duck, Mallard, occasional Bald Eagle)
- Flycatchers working the lake edges in summer
- Less productive for warblers

## How to bird Montgomery Bell (April)

**Arrive by 6:30am.** Dawn chorus is the whole show. By 10am, most songbirds have gone quiet.

**Listen more than look.** Most of these forest birds are in the canopy or understory tangles. You'll hear 5 species for every one you see.

**Bring a field guide or Merlin.** Your phone's cell signal is spotty inside the park. Download the Merlin app's Eastern U.S. bird pack before you arrive.

**Prioritize mnemonics:**
- **"Weeta-weeta-weet-tee-OH"** → Hooded Warbler
- **"Ee-oh-lay"** (flute-like, echoing) → Wood Thrush
- **"Churry-churry-churry"** → Kentucky Warbler
- **Buzzy insect-like trill** → Worm-eating Warbler
- **Loud ringing "twee-twee-twee-twee"** → Louisiana Waterthrush (from creek)
- **Explosive "PEE-ZAY!"** → Acadian Flycatcher
- **Burry "three-eight, three-eight"** → Yellow-throated Vireo
- **"Gobble-gobble-gobble"** → Wild Turkey (you know this one)

**Check the lakes around 8–9am** for wood ducks and migrant waterfowl.

## What to pack

- Binoculars
- Merlin app with Eastern U.S. pack pre-downloaded
- Water + snacks (distances are longer than you think)
- Layered clothing (early April morning can be 40°F; afternoon can hit 75°F)
- Tick protection (permethrin treatment + tuck pants into socks — upland hardwood ticks are fierce)
- Rain layer (Tennessee spring = unpredictable)

## Logistics

- **Where**: Burns, TN — off I-40 exit 182, then US-70 west ~8 miles
- **Admission**: Free
- **Park office**: opens 8am (trail maps, bird checklists)
- **Lodging if staying overnight**: Montgomery Bell Inn on-site, or rent a cabin

## Why these species aren't typical in Memphis

| Species | Memphis? | Why not common here |
|---|---|---|
| Wood Thrush | Migrant only | Needs **large mature interior forest** — Memphis forests are too fragmented |
| Hooded Warbler | Some migration | Needs **dense upland shrub layer** — scarce in Memphis bottomlands |
| Worm-eating Warbler | Rare migrant | Needs **dry ridges + mountain laurel** — Memphis has neither |
| Kentucky Warbler | Occasional (Wolf River) | Needs **moist upland hollows** — more common in MB's ravines |
| Louisiana Waterthrush | Some on Meeman-Shelby bluffs | Needs **rocky gravel streams** — Memphis has muddy bottomland streams instead |
| Yellow-throated Vireo | Rare | Prefers **mature upland hardwood canopy** |
| Acadian Flycatcher | Along Wolf River | More abundant in **hemlock ravines** |
| Wild Turkey | Absent in metro | Needs **contiguous upland forest** — Memphis is too urban |
