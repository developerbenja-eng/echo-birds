# Standing Stone State Park

## Why Standing Stone?

**11,000 acres** of upland forest on the **plateau transition zone** between the Eastern Highland Rim and the Cumberland Plateau — making it one of the **largest state parks in Middle Tennessee**. The CCC-era lake (69 acres), old-growth forest remnants, and remote location produce excellent **breeding warbler diversity** in summer.

Named for a 12-foot standing stone (now destroyed) that marked the boundary between Cherokee and Shawnee hunting grounds.

## The landscape

- **Standing Stone Lake** — 69-acre CCC-era lake (1939)
- **Upland hardwood forest** — extensive oak-hickory-tulip poplar. Some old-growth remnants
- **Plateau transition** — park sits at ~1,800 ft elevation, approaching Cumberland Plateau
- **Streams + ravines** — supporting ravine-specialist species

## Birding routes

### Lake Trail (5 mi loop — moderate)
Circles Standing Stone Lake through mature forest.

- **Hooded Warbler** — dense understory patches, "weeta-weeta-wee-TEE-o"
- **Wood Thrush** — cove forest, ethereal dawn song
- **Northern Parula** — canopy above trail
- **Belted Kingfisher** — lake shoreline

### Overton Lodge Trail (2 mi — easy)
Through upland forest near the lodge.

- **Great Crested Flycatcher** — canopy WHEEP
- **Wild Turkey** — dawn encounters on forest roads
- **Eastern Towhee** — brushy edges, "drink-your-tea"
- **Pileated Woodpecker** — large mature trees

### Forest roads at dawn
Drive the park's gravel roads slowly at sunrise.

- **Wild Turkey** — on roads
- **White-tailed Deer** — very common
- **Ruffed Grouse** — rare but reported (plateau population)

## Seasonal strategy

**May–July** (★★★★): Peak breeding. All forest species singing. Remote + quiet — excellent dawn chorus.

**April** (★★★): Early breeders. Migration adds diversity.

**October** (★★★): Fall foliage at elevation. Late migrants.

**Winter** (★★): Quiet. Winter finches possible in irruption years.

## Logistics

- **Hours**: 7am–sunset
- **Entry**: Free
- **Camping**: 36 sites + cabins + lodge
- **Trails**: 10+ miles
- **Cell service**: Poor (rural Overton County)
- **eBird hotspot**: Standing Stone State Park

**Best strategy**: Full morning — walk lake trail at dawn, then drive forest roads. 4 hours. Remote enough to justify overnight camping.
